About $RSS3

Why the token exists, who keeps the ecosystem running, and how long-term allocation was structured from genesis onward.

$RSS3 is the native token of the RSS3 ecosystem. It was introduced as a coordination layer for the network: a link to connect infrastructure, participation, and ecosystem growth.

RSSHub makes open information accessible. Folo makes it usable in everyday life. Together, they are key parts of the ecosystem. But products alone are not enough to sustain an open information system.

This ecosystem depends on many different roles. Some people maintain routes so data keeps flowing. Some operate instances that keep infrastructure running. Developers build applications and protocols on top. Others organize information into usable forms. Users create demand through reading, subscribing, filtering, and engaging with what they find valuable.

All of this activity moves the system forward. But the value it generates tends to gather at a few points, usually at the application layer. The people who keep the underlying infrastructure alive are rarely the ones who benefit from the growth that happens above it. Over time, maintenance slows down, infrastructure becomes less reliable, and the system weakens. This was a structural problem in the original RSS ecosystem. It proved that open information could reach massive scale, but it had no structure to sustain itself.

This is the problem $RSS3 is designed to address.

The RSS3 ecosystem already leaves a visible record of the work that keeps it running. Route maintenance can be tracked. Instance uptime can be measured. Application usage and protocol activity can also be seen. That makes it possible to build a shared layer across RSSHub, Folo, and the protocols and applications growing around them, so the people who sustain the ecosystem can also participate in the value it creates.

Over time, $RSS3 will be integrated into both existing and future products across the ecosystem. RSSHub and Folo are the first places this begins. As new products are introduced, they can become part of the same system as well. The specific forms of integration will follow the pace of product development.

The principle is straightforward: open information infrastructure should be sustainable, and the people who build and maintain it should share in what it creates.
